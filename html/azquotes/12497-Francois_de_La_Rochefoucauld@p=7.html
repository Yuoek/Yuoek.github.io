Francois de La Rochefoucauld Quotes - Page 7

To be a great man it is necessary to know how to profit by the whole of our good fortune.

Every one speaks well of his own heart, but no one dares speak well of his own mind.

The secret of pleasing in conversation is not to explain too much everything; to say them half and leave a little for divination is a mark of the good opinion we have of others, and nothing flatters their self-love more.

How deceitful hope may be, yet she carries us on pleasantly to the end of life.

We sometimes differ more widely from ourselves than we do from others.

Folly pursues us at all periods of our lives. If someone seems wise it is only because his follies are proportionate to his age and fortune.

A true friend is the most precious of all possessions and the one we take the least thought about acquiring.

Our greediness so often troubles us, making us run after so many things at the same time, that while we too eagerly look after the least we miss the greatest.

Old age is a tyrant, who forbids, under pain of death, the pleasures of youth.

Minds of moderate caliber ordinarily condemn everthing which is beyond their range.

We are never so happy, nor so unhappy, as we suppose ourselves to be.

Before strongly desiring anything, we should look carefully into the happiness of its present owner.

We think very few people sensible, except those who are of our opinion.

Only strong natures can really be sweet ones; those that seem sweet are in general only weak, and may easily turn sour.

Jealousy springs more from love of self than from love of another.

Many people despise wealth, but few know how to give it away.

Love of fame, fear of disgrace, schemes for advancement, desire to make life comfortable and pleasant, and the urge to humiliate others are often at the root of the valour men hold in such high esteem.

Simplicity is a delicate imposition.

As one grows older, one becomes wiser and more foolish.

We are so accustomed to disguise ourselves to others that in the end we become disguised to ourselves.

The qualities we have do not make us so ridiculous as those which we affect to have.
[Fr., On n'est jamais si ridicule par les qualites que l'on a que par celles que l'on affecte d'avoir.]

Sometimes a fool has talent, but never judgment.

Man only blames himself in order that he may be praised.

It is easier to appear worthy of a position one does not hold, than of the office which one fills.

How can we expect another to keep our secret if we have been unable to keep it ourselves?
