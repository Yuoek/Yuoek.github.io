Francois de La Rochefoucauld Quotes - Page 9

Some people displease with merit, and others' very faults and defects are pleasing.

It is the prerogative of great men only to have great defects.

To be a great man it is necessary to turn to account all opportunities.

L'absence diminue les mediocres passions, et augmente les grandes,comme le vent eteint les bougies, et allume le feu. Absence diminishes commonplace passions, and increases great ones, as wind extinguishes candles and kindles fire.

We speak little if not egged on by vanity.

The old begin to complain of the conduct of the young when they themselves are no longer able to set a bad example.

Friendship is insipid to those who have experienced love.

When our vices leave us, we like to imagine it is we who are leaving them.

He who lives without folly isn't so wise as he thinks.

The constancy of the wise is only the talent of concealing the agitation of their hearts.

The heat of youth is not more opposed to safety than the coldness of age.

Jealousy is in some measure just and reasonable, since it merely aims at keeping something that belongs to us or we think belongsto us, whereas envy is a frenzy that cannot bear anything that belongs to others.

We should often feel ashamed of our best actions if the world could see all the motives which produced them.

The tranquility or agitation of our temper does not depend so much on the big things which happen to us in life, as on the pleasant or unpleasant arrangements of the little things which happen daily.

We acknowledge that we should not talk of our wives; but we seem not to know that we should talk still less of ourselves.

They that apply themselves to trifling matters commonly become incapable of great ones.

Self-interest makes some people blind, and others sharp-sighted.

Some good qualities are like the senses: Those who are entirely deprived of them can have no notion of them.

It is the habit of mediocre minds to condemn all that is beyond their grasp.

There are bad people who would be less dangerous if they were quite devoid of goodness.

Some crimes get honor and renown by being committed with more pomp, by a greater number, and in a higher degree of wickedness thanothers. Hence it is that public robberies, plunderings, and sackings have been looked upon as excellencies and noble achievements, and the seizing of whole countries, however unjustly and barbarously, is dignified with the glorious name of gaining conquests.

Fortune never seems so blind to any as to those on whom she bestows no favors.

Ordinary men commonly condemn what is beyond them.

True bravery means doing alone that which one could do if all the world were by.

When the heart is still disturbed by the relics of a passion it is proner to take up a new one than when wholly cured.
