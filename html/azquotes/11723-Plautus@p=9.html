Plautus Quotes - Page 9

Keep what you have got; the known evil is best.
[Lat., Habeas ut nactus; nota mala res optima est.]

Out of many evils the evil which is least is the least of evils.
[Lat., E malis multis, malum, quod minimum est, id minimum est malum.]

If you squander on a holyday, you will want on a workday unless you have been sparing.

We are pouring our words into a sieve, and lose our labor.
[Lat., In pertusum ingerimus dicta dolium, operam ludimus.]

It is easy to rule over the good.

He whom the gods love dies young, whilst he is full of health, perception, and judgment.
[Lat., Quem dii diligunt,
Adolescens moritur, dum valet, sentit, sapit.]

Bad conduct soils the finest ornament more than filth.

I count him lost, who is lost to shame.

Modesty should accompany youth.

We should try to succeed by merit, not by favor. He who does well will always have patrons enough.
[Lat., Virtute ambire oportet, non favitoribus.
Sat habet favitorum semper, qui recte facit.]

That least pleases us which is most urged on us.

He that's in love, i' faith, even if he is hungry, isn't hungry at all.

The sea is certainly common to all.

I trust no rich man who is officiously kind to a poor man.

It wasn't for nothing that the raven was just now croaking on my left hand.

Slander-mongers and those who listen to slander, if I had my way, would all be strung up, the talkers by the tongue, the listeners by the ears.

Find me a reasonable lover against his weight in gold.

A woman smells well when she smells of nothing.

It does not matter a feather whether a man be supported by patron or client, if he himself wants courage.
[Lat., Animus tamen omnia vincit.
Ille etiam vires corpus habere facit.]

To love is human, it is also human to forgive.

Man is a wolf to man.
[Lat., Homo homini lupus.]

It is common to forget a man and slight him if his good will cannot help you.

It well becomes a young man to be modest.

Nothing is there more friendly to a man than a friend in need.

It is well for one to know more than he says.
