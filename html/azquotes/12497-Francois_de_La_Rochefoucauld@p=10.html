Francois de La Rochefoucauld Quotes - Page 10

Idleness and fear keeps us in the path of duty, but our virtue often gets the praise.

Good taste comes more from the judgment than from the mind.

None but the contemptible are apprehensive of contempt.

To understand matters rightly we should understand their details; and as that knowledge is almost infinite, our knowledge is always superficial and imperfect.

Youth is a perpetual intoxication; it is a fever of the mind.

The blindness of men is the most dangerous effect of their pride; it seems to nourish and augment it; it deprives them of knowledge of remedies which can solace their miseries and can cure their faults.

Envy is more irreconcilable than hatred.

Men are not only prone to forget benefits; they even hate those who have obliged them, and cease to hate those who have injured them. The necessity of revenging an injury, or of recompensing a benefit seems a slavery to which they are unwilling to submit.

In all aspects of life, we take on a part and an appearance to seem to be what we wish to be--and thus the world is merely composed of actors.

We should often blush at our noblest deeds if the world were to see all their underlying motives.

Decency is the least of all laws, but yet it is the law which is most strictly observed.

The art of putting into play mediocre qualities often begets more reputation than is achieved by true merit.

Propriety is the least of all laws, and the most observed.

If a man doesn't find ease in himself, 'tis in vain to seek it elsewhere.

All the passions are nothing else than different degrees of heat and cold of the blood.

Whatever ignominy or disgrace we have incurred, it is almost always in our power to reestablish our reputation.

To establish ourselves in the world, we have to do all we can to appear established. To succeed in the world, we do everything we can to appear successful.

Sometimes there are accidents in our lives the skillful extrication from which demands a little folly.

We are sometimes as different from ourselves as we are from others.

More men are guilty of treason through weakness than any studied design to betray.

We have no patience with other people's vanity because it is offensive to our own.

We cannot possibly imagine the variety of contradictions in every heart.

A resolution never to deceive exposes a man to be often deceived.

We torment ourselves rather to make it appear that we are happy than to become so.

If one judges love by the majority of its effects, it is more like hatred than like friendship.
