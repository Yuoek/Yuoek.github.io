While we are reasoning concerning life, life is gone.

It is an absurdity to believe that the Deity has human passions, and one of the lowest of human passions, a restless appetite for applause

The Christian religion not only was at first attended with miracles, but even at this day cannot be believed by any reasonable person without one.

Delicacy of taste has the same effect as delicacy of passion; it enlarges the sphere both of our happiness and our misery.

Human Nature is the only science of man; and yet has been hitherto the most neglected.

All this creative power of the mind amounts to no more than the faculty of compounding, transposing, augmenting, or diminishing the materials afforded us the by senses and experience.

Reason is, and ought only to be the slave of the passions.

Beauty, whether moral or natural, is felt, more properly than perceived.

The heart of man is made to reconcile the most glaring contradictions.

No quality of human nature is more remarkable, both in itself and in its consequences, than that propensity we have to sympathize with others, and to receive by communication their inclinations and sentiments, however different from, or even contrary to our own.

Time is a perishable commodity.

And what is the greatest number? Number one.

The Crusades - the most signal and most durable monument of human folly that has yet appeared in any age or nation.

The most pernicious of all taxes are the arbitrary.

Obscurity, indeed, is painful to the mind as well as to the eye; but to bring light from obscurity, by whatever labour, must needsbe delightful and rejoicing.

A propensity to hope and joy is real riches; one to fear and sorrow real poverty.

The bigotry of theologians is a malady which seems almost incurable.

From the apparent usefulness of the social virtues, it has readily been inferred by sceptics, both ancient and modern, that all moral distinctions arise from education, and were, at first, invented, and afterwards encouraged ... in order to render men tractable, and subdue their natural ferocity and selfishness, which incapacitated them for society.

The richest genius, like the most fertile soil, when uncultivated, shoots up into the rankest weeds.

It is with books as with women, where a certain plainness of manner and of dress is more engaging than that glare of paint and airs and apparel which may dazzle the eye, but reaches not the affections.

Superstition is an enemy to civil liberty.

Habit may lead us to belief and expectation but not to the knowledge, and still less to the understanding, of lawful relations.

For my part, when I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always stumble on some particular perception or other, of heat or cold, light or shade, love or hatred, pain or pleasure. I never can catch myself at any time without a perception, and never can observe anything but the perception.

The great end of all human industry is the attainment of happiness

Great pleasures are much less frequent than great pains.
