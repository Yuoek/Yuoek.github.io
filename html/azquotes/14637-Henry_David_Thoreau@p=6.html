Henry David Thoreau Quotes - Page 6

It is what a man thinks of himself that really determines his fate.

He enjoys true leisure who has time to improve his soul's estate.

Tough times don't last but tough people do.
No matter how slow you go, you are still lapping everybody on the couch.
Men are born to succeed, not fail.

It is best to avoid the beginnings of evil.

A truly good book teaches me better than to read it. I must soon lay it down, and commence living on its hint. What I began by reading, I must finish by acting.

You must speak loud to those who are hard of hearing.

A friend is one who incessantly pays us the compliment of expecting from us all the virtues, and who can appreciate them in us. The friend asks no return but that his friend will religiously accept and wear and not disgrace his apotheosis of him. They cherish each other's hopes. They are kind to each other's dreams.

Wealth can't buy heath, but heath can buy wealth.

You must live in the present, launch yourself on every wave, find your eternity in each moment.

Sweep away the clutter of things that complicate our lives.

It is better to have your head in the clouds, and know where you are... than to breathe the clearer atmosphere below them, and think that you are in paradise.

The little things in life are as interesting as the big ones.

I have no doubt that it is part of the destiny of the human race in its gradual improvement, to leave off eating animals.

Could a greater miracle take place than for us to look through each other's eyes for an instant?

I believe that there is a subtle magnetism in Nature, which, if we unconsciously yield to it, will direct us aright.

It is only when we forget all our learning that we begin to know.

That government is best which governs the least, because its people discipline themselves.

A minority is powerless while it conforms to the majority.

When you knock, ask to see God — none of the servants.

You cannot perceive beauty but with a serene mind.

Birds never sing in caves.

Sobriety, severity, and self-respect are the foundations of all true sociality.

There is nothing more difficult to find than oneself.

Duty is one and invariable; it requires no impossibilities, nor can it ever be disregarded with impunity.

The truth is, there is money buried everywhere, and you have only to go to work to find it.
