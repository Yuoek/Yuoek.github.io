Aung San Suu Kyi Quotes - Page 10

Of course I regret not having been able to spend time with my family.

Absolute peace in our world is an unattainable goal. But it is one towards which we must continue to journey, our eyes fixed on it as a traveller in a desert fixes his eyes on the one guiding star that will lead him to salvation.

What I have experienced is nothing compared to what political prisoners in prisons suffer.

The judiciary must be strengthened and released from political interference.

Perhaps Singapore could learn from us a more relaxed way of life.

One prisoner of conscience is one too many.

It is his capacity for self-improvement and self-redemption which most distinguishes man from the mere brute.

I haven't heard any music on the BBC World Service in a long time. Maybe I'm listening at the wrong times. But not one single piece of music.

After I was released, people used to keep asking me, 'what's it like to be free? And it was very difficult for me to answer. I'd always felt free. As far as my state of mind was concerned, I didn't feel any different...People ask me about what sacrifices I've made. I always answer: I've made no sacrifices, I've made choices.

Assuming the chairmanship of ASEAN isn't going to do anything about improving the lives of people.

The Nobel Peace Prize opened up a door in my heart.

I could listen to the radio and I had access to books from time to time. Not all the time.

Burma wants to have good relations with our neighboring countries, China and India. I do believe the United States itself wants to live in harmony with China and India. That's why we have to lay down political policies that are fair for everyone.

It is incorrect to say there have been no reforms at all in Burma; there have of course been reforms, but we still need to do more for the people. To become a democratic society we have to continually be reforming.

There should be no political prisoners in a democratic country.

Burma is not yet a full-fledged democracy. We have started working on the road to full democracy. We have a lot of things to do in order to build a democratic structure and to be become a full-fledged democracy.

There can be no civil peace without the rule of law.

Foreign investors will not come and do things without any returns for themselves. And we don't expect them to come work for us free. But the local population should benefit rightfully. The locals should benefit just a little more than the companies.

Burmese political culture lacks an understanding of negotiated compromise. We have to build up that kind of culture.

The important thing is Burma needs to end the civil war and for this to happen both sides must want to end the war.

If suffering were an unavoidable part of our existence, we should try to alleviate it as far as possible in practical, earthly ways.

India and Burma have been close friends since the days we were struggling for independence. And I'm a great admirer of Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru, and all those leaders of India's independence movement. I would like to believe the aspirations and hopes we shared in the past will continue to bind us in the future.

I have been arrested time and time again, and my colleagues, too. I cannot guarantee that I'll not be arrested again. But it's not something that weighs on my mind. I'll do what I can while I'm free. If they arrest me again, I'll do what I can while I'm under arrest.

I do not look upon my life as a sacrifice at all.

A revolution simply means great change, significant change, and that's how I'm defining it - great change for the better, brought about through non-violent means.
