Napoleon Bonaparte Quotes - Page 10

Space we can recover; time never.

In war there is but one favorable moment; the great art is to seize it!

What is the government? Nothing, unless supported by opinion.

Insubordination may only be the evidence of a strong mind.

Vengeance is without foresight.

Throw off your worries when you throw off your clothes at night.

I like honest men of all colors.

My true glory is not to have won 40 battles ... Waterloo will erase the memory of so many victories, ... But ... what will live forever, is my Civil Code.

War is the business of barbarians.

Treason is a matter of dates.

I have only one counsel for you - be master.

The only victory over love is flight.

A journalist is a grumbler, a censurer, a giver of advice, a regent of sovereigns, a tutor of nations. Four hostile newspapers are more to be feared than a thousand bayonets.

There is a joy in danger.

Speeches pass away, but acts remain.

You must not fear death, my lads; defy him, and you drive him into the enemy's ranks.

To do all that one is able to do, is to be a man; to do all that one would like to do, is to be a god.

Has a man the right to kill himself? Yes, if his death harms no one and if life is an evil to him. When is life an evil? When it offers a man nothing but suffering and pain.

Public opinion is a mysterious and invisible power, to which everything must yield. There is nothing more fickle, more vague, or more powerful; yet capricious as it is, it is nevertheless much more often true, reasonable, and just, than we imagine.

Glory is fleeting, but obscurity is forever.

When your enemy is doing something wrong, do not interrupt him.

Washington is dead! This great man fought against Tyranny; he established the liberty of his country. His memory will always be dear to the French people, as it will be to all free men of the two worlds; and especially to French soldiers, who, like him and the American soldiers, have combated for liberty and equality.

Among so many conflicting ideas and so many different perspectives, the honest man is confused and distressed and the skeptic becomes wicked ... Since one must take sides, one might as well choose the side that is victorious, the side which devastates, loots, and burns. Considering the alternative, it is better to eat than to be eaten.

The barbarous custom of having men beaten who are suspected of having important secrets to reveal must be abolished. It has always been recognized that this way of interrogating men, by putting them to torture, produces nothing worthwhile.

Men soon get tired of shedding their blood for the advantage of a few individuals, who think they amply reward the soldiers perils with the treasures they amass.
