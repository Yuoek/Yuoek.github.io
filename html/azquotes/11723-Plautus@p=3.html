Plautus Quotes - Page 3

Practice yourself what you preach.

It is not without a purpose when a rich man greets a poor one with kindness.

I regard that man as lost, who has lost his sense of shame.

A well-balanced mind is the best remedy against affliction.

It is a bitter disappointment when you have sown benefits, to reap injuries.

No man has perpetual good fortune.
[Lat., Nulli est homini perpetuum bonum.]

Man is no man, but a wolf

Feast to-day makes fast to-morrow.
[Lat., Festo die si quid prodegeris,
Profesto egere liceat nisi peperceris.]

'He means well' is useless unless he does well.

Courage is to take hard knocks like a man when occasion calls.

You must spend money, if you wish to make money.

No blessing lasts forever.

Every one can remember that which has interested himself.

Never speak ill of an absent friend.

If you are content, you have enough to live comfortably.

In everything the middle road is best.

If you are wise, be wise; keep what goods the gods provide you.

A word to the wise is sufficient

He gains wisdom in a happy way, who gains it by another's experience.
[Lat., Feliciter sapit qui alieno periculo sapit.]

Men understand the worth of blessings only when they have lost them.

He who rushes headlong into love will fare worse than if he had cast himself from a precipice.

Poverty is a thorough instructress in all the arts.
[Lat., Paupertas . . . omnes artes perdocet.]

Good merchandise, even hidden, soon finds buyers.

He who dies for virtue does not perish.

No guest is so welcome in a friend's house that he will not become a nuisance after three days.
